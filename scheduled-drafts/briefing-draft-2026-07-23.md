## ⚡ 1. ERCOT & Grid Signals
**PUC/ERCOT Send Abbott a Legislative Wish List — Foreign-Ownership Grid Safeguard Tops It — While a Senate Hearing and a Batch Zero Deadline Both Land Within a Week**

- The PUC and ERCOT's July 17 joint memo to Governor Abbott, filed publicly Monday and reported yesterday, asks the 2027 Legislature for three new powers: authority for ERCOT to send curtailment orders directly to a data center rather than routing through its local utility; a mandate that large computational loads register with both agencies and hand over technical details; and — the sharpest ask — expanding the 2021 Lone Star Infrastructure Protection Act so it also bars China-, Russia-, Iran-, or North Korea-linked owners from controlling Texas data centers, not just generation and transmission assets.
- The Texas Senate Business and Commerce Committee has set a July 29 hearing (9 a.m., Capitol Extension Room E1.012, Chair Sen. Charles Schwertner) to take testimony on ERCOT reliability, Senate Bill 6's large-load interconnection rules, and the property-rights fight over the state's expanding 765-kV transmission backbone. It's an interim-charge review with no vote, feeding into the 2027 session.
- The utility-side Batch Zero technical package is due to ERCOT tomorrow, July 24; applicant-side submissions closed July 10, with a final deficiency-cure deadline of August 31.
- Abbott's June 10 directive gave PUC and ERCOT until July 17 (already passed) to report back on ratepayer protections, and requires the commission to formally act on shifting large-load interconnection costs off residential bills by July 31 — eight days out. The PUC says a rulemaking toward that goal is already open, with a final decision due under a December 2026 statutory deadline.
- Context on the industry's other new obligation: the PUC's July 9 vote (5-0) to adopt "ride-through" rules — requiring data centers and other large loads to stay connected through grid disturbances or face disconnection — is drawing litigation threats. Texas Blockchain Council members estimate compliance costs of $500,000–$1 million per megawatt; the Data Center Coalition argues the requirement should have come from the PUC, not ERCOT.

**Today's Signal:** The foreign-ownership safeguard is the header-grabber, but the more consequential near-term fight is procedural — PUC wants ERCOT talking directly to data-center operators during emergencies, not through utilities, which would meaningfully expand ERCOT's day-to-day authority over these facilities ahead of a Legislature that convenes in six months.

## 🏗️ 2. Data Center Construction & Corporate Activity
**BlackRock Leads a $12B+ Bond Sale for Meta's El Paso Campus — No New DFW-Specific Filing Surfaced Today**

- BlackRock (via Global Infrastructure Partners and HPS Investment Partners) is leading a bond sale targeting more than $12 billion to finance Meta's El Paso data-center campus, arranged by JPMorgan Chase and Morgan Stanley, first reported this week by the Wall Street Journal and Bloomberg. Multiple reports describe an 80/20 BlackRock/Meta ownership split, structured to keep the debt off BlackRock's own balance sheet.
- No new dated corporate land purchase, interconnection filing, or air-permit application surfaced today specifically inside the DFW metro. The most recent concrete regional signals remain last week's items: the AWS ~$1.2B, four-building Wharton County campus filing (construction start August 1) and the Chevron/Microsoft Project Kilby gas-power arrangement for a 2.7 GW West Texas campus.
- Statewide, Texas now has 335 operating data centers and 247 more planned, according to figures cited in yesterday's PUC/ERCOT legislative memo coverage — a scale regulators are citing directly in their case for more oversight authority.

**Today's Signal:** The BlackRock financing structure — debt-heavy, off-balance-sheet, majority-owned by an asset manager rather than the hyperscaler itself — is becoming the template other Texas mega-campuses are likely to follow; worth watching whether Chevron/Microsoft or the AWS Wharton project surface similar financing arrangements next.

**No New DFW-County Filing Dated Today — Statewide Scrutiny of Texas's Permitting Approach Continues to Build**

- No new dated TCEQ draft permit, technical-complete notice, or public-comment posting specific to Collin, Ellis, Johnson, Denton, Wise, Tarrant, Parker, or Hood counties surfaced today. TCEQ's Title V pending-applications list (last updated July 19) still shows Johnson and Ellis county filings dated April and earlier this month, with nothing new added since.
- The backdrop keeps building regardless: a legal-industry analysis published roughly a week ago ("The Great Power Secession," JD Supra) argues nearly half of all newly proposed Texas gas-plant capacity is now earmarked to power data centers directly, calling the state's minor-source permitting approach for these facilities out as a national outlier.
- The Texas Tribune/Floodlight review published earlier this month remains the most-cited statewide figure in current coverage: nine reviewed data-center-linked gas permits could together emit more than 130 million tons of greenhouse gases annually.
- All eight named DFW-area counties remain under TCEQ Region 4 and the DFW nine-county "serious" ozone nonattainment designation (effective since July 2024, attainment deadline August 2027), which continues to raise the compliance bar for any new gas-fired permit application in the region.

**Today's Signal:** With no new DFW-specific filing today, the section to watch is procedural rather than local — whether the EPA's minor-source rule rollback (still open for comment through August 21) ends up making it easier or harder for the next DFW-area gas-fired application to clear TCEQ review.

## 🏛️ 4. Land-Use & Zoning Developments
**Fort Worth's August 11 Vote and Denton's August Moratorium Discussion Remain the Two Dates to Watch — No New Filing Today**

- Fort Worth: no new filing or vote occurred today. The standoff set up last week remains unresolved — the Zoning Commission voted 7-4 on July 8 against staff's proposed data-center standards (a 250-foot residential buffer, noise mitigation, closed-loop cooling, and a ban on crypto-mining facilities), sending the ordinance back to city council. Five council members (Beck, Flores, Hall, Martinez, Nettles) responded July 9 by calling for a temporary moratorium instead. Both threads converge at the council's August 11 meeting.
- Denton: City Manager-adjacent officials (per KERA's July 13 report, citing Mayor Pro Tem Nick Stevens) say the council will take up a possible AI data-center moratorium "sometime in August," with no formal vote date yet set. That follows the council's unanimous June 5 vote directing staff to begin researching a moratorium, and the standing-room-only July 16 town hall hosted with DSA Denton.
- No new zoning filing, industrial/warehouse proposal, or rural land purchase near a substation or transmission corridor surfaced today for Parker, Hood, or the other outlying counties tracked in prior briefings.

**Today's Signal:** Fort Worth and Denton are both now explicitly parked on August council dates — worth treating the next three weeks as a quiet stretch locally, with the real DFW zoning news likely to land in the August 4–11 window.
